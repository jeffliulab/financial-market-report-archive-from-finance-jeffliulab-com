## 🌍 Global Headlines

**Iran Ceasefire Holds but Tensions Smolder in the Gulf**  
A fragile ceasefire between Iran and its adversaries appears to be holding, yet the region remains on edge after Iranian drones struck a South Korean oil tanker in the Strait of Hormuz. Tehran has simultaneously warned the US against attacking its tankers and threatened a “decisive response” to British warships deployed to the area. This volatile mix suggests both sides are testing the ceasefire’s limits while avoiding outright collapse; the maritime attack raises the risk of a broader escalation that could disrupt global oil supply lines and draw in more naval powers. The incident also underscores the challenge of controlling proxy forces, as any sustained disruption would send crude prices sharply higher—a scenario that now looms with oil already at $98.15 per barrel.

**Russia-Ukraine Ceasefire Shattered by Mutual Drone Accusations**  
Deadly drone strikes have shattered the precarious ceasefire between Moscow and Kyiv, with both capitals trading blame. *The Economist* reports that Russia is “stumbling on the battlefield” even as former German chancellor Gerhard Schröder was named by Putin to join Ukraine talks, triggering a “no comment” from Berlin. The strikes expose the ceasefire’s deep fragility, while Schröder’s involvement—though controversial—signals that backchannel diplomacy remains alive, albeit unlikely to gain traction without a foundation of trust. The breakdown on the eastern front adds another layer of uncertainty to European security and could accelerate NATO’s force posture adjustments.

**EU Edges Closer to Sanctioning West Bank Settlers Amid Land Grabs**  
The EU’s top diplomat is “hopeful” that the bloc will approve sanctions against violent Israeli settlers today, as a Palestinian family was forced to exhume their father’s remains for a settlement expansion. This would mark a significant hardening of European policy, but implementation faces legal hurdles and Israeli pushback. The forced exhumation underscores the accelerating reality of West Bank annexation, placing settler violence at the center of EU-Israel tensions. For Washington, the move creates a quiet diplomatic pressure point: the Biden administration has long resisted labeling settler violence as a barrier to peace, but may now be forced to calibrate its response to avoid a transatlantic rift.

**UK’s Starmer Fights for Political Survival, Pivots to Europe**  
Prime Minister Keir Starmer launches a political fightback by placing European ties at the heart of a government reset, as Labour MPs are urged not to topple him. This foreign-policy gambit aims to regain domestic credibility amid internal dissent, but it risks appearing as a distraction from economic stagnation. The coming weeks will test whether a pro-Europe agenda can unify a fractious party or deepen its divisions. The irony is that a leader once seen as a safe pair of hands is now leaning on Brussels for political lifeline—a strategy that may appeal to centrist voters but could alienate the left wing of his party.

**Thailand’s Thaksin Shinawatra Free—Rest of Region Watches**  
Thaksin Shinawatra is out of prison, raising questions about his political comeback and its impact on Thailand’s fragile democratic transition. His release could bolster the Pheu Thai Party’s hold on power, but also risks fanning a backlash from royalist-military forces. For Southeast Asia, Thaksin’s next move—whether he enters active politics or plays kingmaker—will shape the stability of a key ASEAN economy. Beijing, a major investor in Thailand’s Belt and Road infrastructure, will be watching closely: a stable Bangkok is critical for Chinese regional ambitions.

**Hungary’s Symbolic Culture Shift Amid Geopolitical Drift**  
A politician’s viral dance moves are being read as a marker of a more optimistic Hungarian mood, according to *The Guardian*. While superficially trivial, the episode reflects a public yearning for lighter politics after years of EU friction and authoritarian drift. Whether this translates into real policy openings—especially on rule-of-law disputes with Brussels—remains uncertain, but it is a reminder that even in a politically strained EU member, cultural signals can presage broader shifts.

**Hantavirus Case Spooks Maritime Health Protocols**  
A French national returning from a hantavirus-hit ship has developed symptoms, raising fresh concerns about disease surveillance on international vessels. Although isolated, the incident could prompt tighter shipping sanitation rules and remind global health officials that biological risks travel as fast as trade. It also highlights the challenge of tracking outbreaks in the loosely regulated maritime sector, where crew movement and port calls create a complex web of potential transmission.

## 🇺🇸 United States

**Iran Ceasefire Lingers, but $5 Gas Haunts the White House**  
The fragile Iran ceasefire—now in its second week—has prevented full-scale war, but is not yet stabilizing oil markets. With Iran warning the US against attacking its tankers and drones hitting a South Korean ship, the specter of $5-per-gallon gasoline remains a political vulnerability for the Biden administration. The White House faces a difficult balancing act: maintain enough pressure on Tehran to enforce the ceasefire while avoiding any action that could spike energy prices ahead of midterm primaries. The VIX’s jump to 18.20 on Monday suggests that markets are pricing in a non-trivial chance of the ceasefire unraveling, which would throw crude into triple-digit territory.

**U.S. Relies on Allies for Hormuz Deterrence**  
The deployment of British warships to the Strait of Hormuz, coupled with Iran’s “decisive response” threat, highlights the US’s preference for burden-sharing in the region. Washington is unlikely to commit large naval assets directly, instead allowing allies to front-line deterrence. This approach may reduce US exposure but risks fragmenting coalition response if Iran interprets the shared force as weaker. The South Korean tanker attack also puts pressure on Seoul to join the maritime coalition, which would further multilateralize the deterrent but also complicate diplomatic off-ramps with Tehran.

**No Major Domestic Policy Dramas, but Subplots Brew**  
The absence of major US domestic headlines—no Fed guidance, no legislative breakthroughs—belies simmering issues. The forced exhumation of a Palestinian father by Israeli settlers, while not a US story, demands a quiet response from Washington, which has long resisted labeling settler violence as a barrier to peace. Expect the administration to privately press Israel while publicly avoiding ruptures. Meanwhile, the quiet regulatory calendar on tech and finance may be the calm before a storm: with European moves on AI governance and digital markets gaining steam, American firms are watching for any precedent that could ripple back across the Atlantic later in the year.

## 🇨🇳 China & Asia-Pacific

**Iran Crisis Imperils China’s Oil Lifeline**  
The drone strike on a South Korean tanker in the Strait of Hormuz and continued Iran ceasefire fragility threaten China’s crude supply, which relies heavily on Middle East flows. Beijing likely leans on its diplomatic ties with Tehran to push for a lasting truce, but may also quietly increase strategic petroleum reserve draws. Any sustained disruption would force China to calibrate its energy security posture and could accelerate diversification toward Russian and Central Asian pipelines. The timing is awkward: China’s economic recovery is still nascent, and a sustained oil price spike would squeeze margins for manufacturers and raise inflation risks just as the People’s Bank of China tries to stimulate domestic demand.

**Thaksin’s Release Reshapes Southeast Asian Politics**  
Thaksin Shinawatra’s release from prison is the biggest political development in Thailand since his 2006 coup. His return could stabilize the Pheu Thai-led government by unifying pro-Thaksin factions, but also risks provoking a new cycle of confrontation with the military and monarchy. For ASEAN, Thailand’s internal stability is critical; a protracted political crisis would undermine the bloc’s unity and China’s Belt and Road investments in the country. Markets initially reacted cautiously, with the Thai baht steady but investors waiting for Thaksin’s first public moves.

**South Korean Ship Attack: A New Type of Maritime Threat**  
Iranian drones striking a South Korean vessel in the Hormuz strait marks a dangerous escalation targeting a US ally’s commercial shipping. Seoul will likely seek naval protection from Washington and may tighten ties with the US-led maritime coalition. This incident also tests the limits of the Iran ceasefire: if Tehran cannot control its proxies, the broader truce could unravel. For the Asia-Pacific region, the attack serves as a chilling reminder that the war at sea has moved beyond tanker seizures and into precision drone-strike territory.

**Japan’s Tech Sector Under Pressure**  
While Nintendo’s share tumble (detailed below) is a corporate story, it reflects broader Japanese tech sector anxiety about hardware cycles and innovation lag. The Nikkei 225’s 0.47% decline on Monday, contrasting with gains in China and the US, suggests investors are rotating away from Japan’s legacy electronics firms toward AI beneficiaries elsewhere. The market’s harsh reaction to Nintendo’s Switch 2 pipeline signals that Japan’s entertainment giants cannot rely on nostalgia alone; they need to accelerate digital transformation to stay competitive with Chinese and American rivals.
